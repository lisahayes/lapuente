Hidden Water Leak Detector

Hidden leaks announce themselves in fragments: a sound here, a bill there, a smell nobody can place. This screener collects the fragments. Check every sign that applies to your La Puente home right now, and the score converts scattered clues into a likelihood tier with the correct next move. The water meter moves with every fixture in the house turned off (the fifteen-minute test)

Why these eight signs, in this order

The checklist mirrors how professional triage actually weighs evidence. Meter movement with the house at rest is direct proof of pressurized loss, which is why it carries the heaviest weight and why a checked first box escalates any score. Warm floors and wall hiss are near-direct: physical evidence of water moving where it should not, strongly associated with the hot-side slab and pinhole failures La Puente’s boom-era copper produces. The remaining five are corroboration, real signals that earn attention in combination even when each alone has innocent explanations.

A high score is not a diagnosis; it is a well-founded suspicion, which is exactly what a detection visit converts into a marked point and priced options.

Reading your tier honestly

Low-tier households should resist both panic and complacency: date the observation, keep the monthly meter habit, and let time either clear or convict. Elevated tiers have earned the fifteen-minute test tonight, since one checkbox there reshapes the whole verdict. High tiers should treat the score as a same-week phone call, because every pattern in that tier involves water actively moving, and in this city’s slab-on-grade housing, moving water picks its own exit if nobody intervenes.

Whatever the tier, keep the checked list; telling our dispatcher which signs applied starts the visit with the suspect pool already halved.
