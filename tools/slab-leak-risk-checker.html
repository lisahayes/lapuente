Why era and material dominate the weighting

La Puente’s slab leak population is overwhelmingly one demographic: 1950s and 1960s tract homes running original thin-wall copper under slab-on-grade foundations, in basin water hard enough at 10 to 17 grains to have spent six decades pitting that copper from the inside. A house matching that profile carries most of the available risk before any symptom appears, which is why the top two selectors move the score more than anything except live symptoms. Pre-1955 homes score lower here not because they are young but because many are raised-foundation, and their pipes fail above a crawl space instead of under concrete.

History and pressure are the accelerants: prior failures date the metal’s condition directly, and unregulated pressure works every thinned wall harder.

Using the score like an adult

No checker diagnoses a slab; it prioritizes attention. Low and moderate profiles buy calm plus two cheap habits, the monthly meter glance and a known shutoff valve. Elevated profiles add the pressure test and the census mindset about first failures. High profiles with a live symptom should convert the score to a phone call, because the profile plus the symptom is precisely the combination our detection sequence exists to confirm or clear, cheaply, before the water chooses its own exit through your flooring.

Scores also travel: if your tract twin next door just scored high the hard way, your identical house inherited the information.
