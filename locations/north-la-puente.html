One cohort, one failure calendar

North La Puente’s houses were plumbed together, which means they age together. The same thin-wall copper, soldered by the same era’s crews into the same slab-on-grade tract plans, has been pitting on the same schedule under the same Main San Gabriel Basin groundwater. When one street starts producing slab leaks and pinholes, its neighbors are not unlucky; they are next on the calendar, and the pattern is visible in our dispatch logs block by block.

That is grim phrased one way and useful phrased another: on the north side, your neighbor’s leak is your early warning, and acting on it is the cheapest plumbing decision this cohort offers.

The two questions every north-side call starts with

Warm spot on the floor: how sure is that, really, before anyone opens concrete?

Very sure by the time concrete is involved, because nothing opens on the warm spot alone. The spot earns a meter test, hot-cold isolation, thermal confirmation of the plume, and an acoustic peak that agrees with all of it, our standard two-confirmations-before-paint, three-before-concrete rule. On this cohort’s hot-side failures, that sequence converges fast, and the opening it produces is a hand-span, not a trench.

First pinhole just happened. Are we really supposed to think about a repipe already?

You are supposed to think with evidence, which the first failure conveniently provides. We cut the failed section lengthwise and show you the remaining wall and the pit field. Clean metal beyond one unlucky pit argues for the spot fix and calm. A wall thinned across the sample argues that leak two is maturing somewhere already, and the reroute-or-repipe math deserves a look on your schedule instead of the water’s.

The services this cohort keeps busy

Three pages on this site are effectively North La Puente’s neighborhood services. Slab leak detection and repair, because every one of these houses runs supply lines under or through its slab and the hot side fails first. Pinhole repair, because pitting corrosion in hard groundwater is the cohort’s signature failure. And whole-house repiping, because for a growing share of these streets the honest math has crossed over from patching to replacing.

The bridge between them is detection discipline: on sixty-year copper, every repair visit doubles as a census of what remains, and you get that census straight.

North-side pattern worth knowing: hot-slab leaks announce as warm floor spots and a water heater that cycles at odd hours. Two symptoms together are close to a diagnosis before we arrive.

Living well on original copper

Plenty of north-side homes will run their original lines for years yet, and the difference between the ones that do and the ones that flood is management. Verified system pressure, since an unregulated spike works every thinned wall at once. Water heater maintenance, because scale and sediment tax the hot side that fails first. And the meter-test habit, fifteen quiet minutes a month, which catches the cohort’s slow failures while they are still cheap.

When the calendar does come due, you will hear it from us as evidence and numbers, not drama. Until then, the line answers around the clock: (626) 898-6169.
