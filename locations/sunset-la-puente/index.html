If you wanted one neighborhood to stand for the whole incorporation boom, Sunset would sit for the portrait: slab-on-grade tract plans, original thin-wall copper, and streets laid out in the same optimistic few years either side of 1956. Predictability as a homeowner’s asset

Sunset’s uniformity is diagnostic gold. Because the streets share one era, one plan, and one water supply running 10 to 17 grains hard, their failures cluster tightly: pinhole pitting on the original 1950s copper leads by a wide margin, hot-side slab leaks follow, and fixture-scale wear runs constant in the background. When a Sunset homeowner describes a symptom, the prior probabilities are already sorted before the truck starts, which shortens detection and keeps repairs in the small category.

The pinhole page is effectively this neighborhood’s biography, and the mist-behind-drywall failure it describes is Sunset’s signature call.

Uniformity also makes prevention unusually measurable here. When we log which block produced this month’s failures, the adjacent blocks share the finding, and a Sunset homeowner who asks what the street has been calling about gets an answer with real predictive value, something scattered-era neighborhoods simply cannot offer.

The questions Sunset asks in year sixty-plus

Every neighbor seems to have had a pinhole. Is ours inevitable?

On original lines, the honest answer is that pitting is a process, not a lottery, and sixty years of hard water have run it everywhere on these streets. What is not inevitable is the damage: households that keep the meter-test habit and act on the first failure’s evidence convert the process into scheduled, modest repairs. The neighbors with horror stories are usually the ones whose first pinhole got a patch and a shrug.

Is there any point softening the water this late?

For the copper’s past, no; consumed wall thickness stays consumed. For everything else, maybe: softening helps fixtures, heater life, and any new pipe a future repipe installs. Think of it as a decision that belongs with the repipe conversation rather than a rescue for the original lines, and size it against real hardness numbers, which we measure rather than assume.

A faucet drips and a wall hisses. Same visit?

Same visit, and that combination is very Sunset: the drip is scale-worn cartridge seats, the hiss deserves the full supply-side workup since a misting pinhole is the neighborhood’s specialty. The faucet rebuild rides along at small cost while the detection gear answers the wall question, and one truck roll covers both.

The two conversations every Sunset house eventually has

Conversation one is management: pressure verified and regulated, water heater flushed against the mineral load, first failures investigated with the pipe sample in hand. Run well, it buys original systems years of calm service. Conversation two is the endgame, and Sunset is where the repipe page gets its steadiest readership: when failures start arriving as a series, the tract’s uniformity means the math is unusually clear, and the project itself runs quick in these one-story plans.
