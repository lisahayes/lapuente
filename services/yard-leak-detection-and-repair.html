The suspect lineup under a wet lawn

Four systems can wet a La Puente yard from below: the domestic service line crossing to the house, the irrigation network webbed everywhere, pool plumbing where a pool exists, and the drain or sewer lateral heading for the street. A fifth author, surface drainage, works from above. Each leaves a different signature. Service-line water is constant and clean, and the meter confesses it. Irrigation water keeps the controller’s schedule, greenest along one lateral. Pool water tracks the pump. Lateral leakage smells like what it is and often grows the healthiest, most suspicious grass on the block.

The signatures point the testing, and the testing convicts a system before a single shovel moves. Yards near the Puente Hills side, where Schabarum Regional Park’s slopes shed winter runoff toward the flats, get the drainage question asked first in wet season and last in dry.

Symptom reading, then system isolation

We start with your yard’s testimony: where it is soft, when it worsens, what schedule it keeps. Then isolation turns testimony into verdict: the meter test with the house valved off screens the service line; running irrigation zone by zone against a still meter assigns any loss to a specific valve circuit; pool systems get their own pressure isolation; and the lateral, if suspected, meets the camera. The wet patch stops being a mystery and becomes an address on a specific pipe.

Only then does location refine to a dig point, with acoustic listening and line tracing marking the spot and depth, so the repair excavation is measured in shovel-widths.

Green stripes are data: turf that outgrows its neighbors in a line is mapping a leaking pipe below, fertilized by whatever the pipe carries. Photograph the stripe before mowing; it is the cheapest leak survey available.

Repairs that end the patch, not just visit it

Each convicted system gets its own correct fix, detailed on its own page: service lines spot-repaired or pulled new, irrigation laterals and valves rebuilt with the design flaw corrected, pool runs re-plumbed at the break, laterals spot-repaired or lined per the camera’s evidence. The yard-level standard we add is restoration discipline: proper bedding and compaction so the repair does not settle into a new low spot, and turf or hardscape closed back cleanly.

Where the author was drainage all along, you get the honest map, grade, gutters, and where the winter water wants to go, instead of a plumbing invoice for the sky’s work.

Water this valley meters and manages

Every gallon a yard leak wastes here is metered basin groundwater a whole region budgets carefully, and drought discipline runs deep in these neighborhoods for good reason. A leak under the lawn undoes a household’s conservation quietly and completely: the shorter showers, the drought-tolerant beds, all offset by a pipe donating to the subsoil around the clock.

Yard questions from the dry-season detectives

The wet spot is nowhere near any pipe I know about. What now?

Yards keep secrets: abandoned laterals, extensions from previous owners, and lines that wander far from the straight paths everyone assumes. Water also travels underground before surfacing, so the wet spot marks an exit, not necessarily the pipe. Electronic tracing maps what actually runs beneath your yard, found lines and forgotten ones alike, and the leak gets located on the real map instead of the remembered one.

Should I stop watering to see if the patch dries?

Yes, and make it a controlled experiment: pause irrigation for several dry days and watch the patch. If it dries, suspicion lands on the irrigation network and we test it zone by zone. If it stays soft through the pause, a constant-pressure source, service line or pool side, moves to the front. Either way you have converted a guess into evidence before the visit, which shortens everything.

Mushrooms and beetles love one corner of the lawn. Diagnostic?

Genuinely, yes. Fungi and moisture-loving insects colonize chronically damp soil, and their persistent presence in one zone of an otherwise dry yard is a long-exposure photograph of subsurface water. It does not name the pipe, but it timestamps the problem as chronic rather than recent, which matters for both the search and the repair scope. Bring the observation to the call; ecology is admissible evidence.
