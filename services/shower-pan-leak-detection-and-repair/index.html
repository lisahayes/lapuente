What a pan is, and what failure means

Beneath the tile you stand on lies the actual waterproofing: a pan assembly, hot-mopped asphalt layers in the older tradition, sheet liners or modern bonded membranes since, sloped to a weep-equipped drain. Tile and grout above it are decorative armor, not the seal; pans are built expecting some water to pass the tile and be carried to the drain by the membrane. Failure means the membrane itself is breached or its drain connection has let go, and water is exiting into the subfloor instead.

That distinction rescues homeowners from the classic misdiagnosis: dark grout or a hairline in tile does not equal a failed pan, and a failed pan can hide under flawless tile.

Running the flood test properly

The protocol is strict because its verdict is expensive either way. The drain is sealed with a test plug below the strainer so the drain connection itself is included in the test. Water fills to a marked depth safely below the curb. The level is monitored against evaporation over a sustained period while moisture readings are taken at the surrounding walls, the curb exterior, and the ceiling or floor cavity below. A dropping level or advancing moisture convicts the pan; a stable test clears it and redirects suspicion to the valve, arm, or enclosure.

We document depth, duration, and readings, which matters when the answer feeds an insurance file or a remodel decision.

A pan flood test is also the correct acceptance test for brand-new shower construction. If a contractor built you a shower, this test is how you verify it before the first real use, not after the first stain.

Era matters: how La Puente pans were built

Original bathrooms in the boom tracts typically carry hot-mop pans that have now served for decades; asphalt membranes age, and their drain connections and corner folds are the statistical failure points. Remodels from later decades introduced vinyl sheet liners, excellent when folded and clamped correctly and unforgiving when not, and the newest work uses bonded surface membranes. Knowing which construction is under your tile calibrates both the test interpretation and the repair conversation.

Homes that have never been remodeled are running membranes from the original build, and a failed flood test there is less a surprise than a retirement announcement.

Repair honesty: patches versus rebuilds

Here is the part we say plainly: a genuinely breached pan membrane is not patched from above. Sealers poured down drains and epoxy skims over grout treat the armor, not the seal, and they fail on schedule. The durable repair rebuilds the pan, tear-out to the membrane, new pan construction to current standards, correctly clamped drain, then tile, and we quote it as the construction project it is. What we also say plainly is when you do not need it, because a cleared flood test means the real leak is cheaper and elsewhere.

Shower pan questions, answered by the test

Can I keep using the shower while deciding about a failed pan?

Every use of a failed pan feeds water into the subfloor and framing, so treat it as closed for business. In multi-bath homes, shift to another shower; otherwise, brief and careful use with a plan already scheduled is a judgment call we will help you make against the moisture readings. What does not work is a season of normal use on a known breach; wood and mold keep the calendar.

The flood test passed but I still get moisture. What now?

Good news wearing a confusing hat: your expensive component is innocent. A passed test moves the investigation upward and outward, to the valve and arm inside the wall, the enclosure seams, the door, or occasionally a neighboring fixture whose water travels. The same visit continues through those suspects, and the pan’s clean bill of health has just saved you a rebuild-sized bill.

How long does a proper pan rebuild take?

Plan in days, not hours: demolition to the membrane, new pan construction with cure and inspection points, its own flood test before tile, then tile and grout with their setting times. The exact span depends on size and materials, and we schedule it in phases announced ahead so the household can plan. It is real construction, which is exactly why the flood test must justify it first.
