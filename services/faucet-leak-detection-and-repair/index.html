What local water does inside a faucet

Every faucet in the San Gabriel Valley runs a gauntlet its designers tested for only politely. Water at 10 to 17 grains per gallon deposits scale on the cartridge surfaces that are supposed to glide, embeds grit in the rubber seats that are supposed to seal, and crusts the aerator screens until flow turns sideways. The result is the familiar local lifecycle: a faucet gets stiff, then weepy, then drippy, years ahead of the same model in a soft-water town.

Around Bassett’s older kitchens we see the full museum: compression faucets on their fourth washer, cartridge singles ground stiff by scale, and quality brass old enough to vote that still deserves rebuilding rather than replacement.

Rebuild or replace: how we call it

Faucets divide into the rebuildable and the disposable, and the honest answer differs by what is on your sink. Solid-brass fixtures from reputable makers rebuild beautifully: new cartridge or stem, fresh seats and springs, cleaned passages, and the faucet runs another decade. Builder-grade units with plastic bodies usually cost more in labor to rebuild than a better replacement costs outright, and we say so instead of billing the theater of a doomed rebuild.

Either way the fix includes the parts people skip: supply lines past their prime, corroded mounting hardware, and the shutoff valves under the sink that must actually shut off before any future repair is a small one.

An aerator unscrews by hand and rinses clean in a minute. In this water it is the single highest-value piece of faucet maintenance a homeowner can do, and it fixes half of all weak-flow complaints on the spot.

The leaks that hide below the deck

Not all faucet leaks exit the spout. Base gaskets weep under the body where nobody looks, spray-hose connections drip inside the cabinet only while the water runs, and a cracked mounting nut lets every use send a teaspoon down the back of the cabinet. These below-deck leaks are why a faucet complaint gets the flashlight treatment: we run the fixture through its motions and inspect underneath while it works.

Persistent under-sink moisture with a dry faucet points the investigation onward to supply connections or the drain assembly, which is a different repair and a direct handoff within the same visit.

Outdoor faucets deserve the same respect

The humble hose bib is a faucet too, and in these neighborhoods it is often the oldest one on the property, threaded into a galvanized stub behind stucco. A bib that drips at the handle or weeps from the stem wastes water in plain sight and, worse, can leak backward into the wall it penetrates. Stem repairs, seat replacement, and full bib upgrades with proper backflow protection are all standard work.

Indoors or out, the pattern holds: small parts, big water, quick fixes when done right. Start with (626) 898-6169.

Faucet questions from hard-water kitchens

How fast does a small drip actually add up?

Faster than intuition suggests. A drip per second is on the order of thousands of gallons a year, all of it metered, and hot-side drips also bill you twice by draining the water heater’s work. The repair is usually one of the least expensive on this site, which makes the dripping faucet the rare leak where procrastination has a precisely measurable price.

My single-handle faucet got stiff and now drips. Same problem?

Almost always, yes: scale on the cartridge. Mineral deposits roughen the surfaces the cartridge slides across, the seals wear against the roughness, and stiffness graduates into dripping. A new cartridge, plus cleaning the scale from the valve body while it is open, restores both the glide and the seal. If the finish and body are decent, this rebuild beats replacement comfortably.

Do you install customer-purchased faucets?

Yes, with one honest caveat: we will tell you what we think of the unit before it goes in, because installation labor is the same for a faucet that lasts fifteen years and one that lasts three. Bring what you like and we install it properly, new supplies and working shutoffs included. Ask before you buy and we will happily point at models that survive this water.
