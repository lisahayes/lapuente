What trenchless actually requires from detection

Open-trench repair tolerates vague diagnosis; you dig until you find it. Trenchless methods tolerate none: lining a lateral, bursting in a new pipe, or pulling a fresh service line all demand knowing the run’s exact route, depth, condition, and defect locations before commitment, because the technique threads new pipe through or alongside old geography. Detection for trenchless is therefore a full survey: camera traversal with a locating sonde logging position and depth at every defect, plus condition grading of the whole run.

The survey’s verdict is binary and honest: this line qualifies for a trenchless path, or its geometry, collapse, severe bellies, back-pitch, disqualifies it and open repair is the truthful quote.

The assessment: grading a pipe for its second career

A lining candidate must hold a liner: continuous enough to traverse, round enough to seat against, and correctly pitched, since lining preserves the host’s slope forever. A bursting candidate needs a route free of unsurvivable fittings and neighbors, because the bursting head splits the old pipe outward as the new one follows. A pull candidate needs entry and exit geometry and a corridor clear of conflicts. Each check happens on camera and locator before any equipment mobilizes, which is why our trenchless quotes do not grow surprises mid-job.

On the lateral runs dropping toward the trunk lines the county sanitation districts maintain, the driveways along the Hacienda Heights border side make the survey’s stakes concrete in both senses.

Entry pits instead of trenches: the payoff

When a line qualifies, the excavation shrinks to punctuation: an entry pit at each end of the run, sometimes only one, instead of a trench along its length. The hardscape between stays whole, the tree roots stay unmet, and restoration costs collapse to two small patches. Detection precision is what purchased that outcome; every foot of route and depth certainty converts directly into feet of yard nobody disturbs.

Where a run only partially qualifies, the hybrid answer is standard: trenchless for the qualifying length, one spot excavation at the disqualifying defect, still a fraction of the full trench.

Trenchless is a detection-dependent discount: the same repair that qualifies with a precise survey becomes an open trench without one. The survey is not a preliminary; it is the technology’s foundation.

Trenchless questions from above the run

My whole yard was quoted for excavation elsewhere. Is trenchless possible instead?

Get the survey before accepting the trench. A meaningful share of runs quoted for full excavation qualify for lining, bursting, or pulling once actually camera-graded and located, because trenching quotes are often written without that data. If your line qualifies, the yard survives; if it genuinely does not, you will at least know why, on camera, rather than on someone’s assurance.

Does lining a pipe reduce its capacity?

The liner takes a small dimensional bite and returns a large hydraulic favor: the new interior is smooth and jointless, so flow typically matches or improves on the aged, rough, root-snagged host it replaces. Capacity questions get answered per line during assessment, since diameter and fixture load are measurable facts, and any run where the math argues against lining hears us say so.

Can trenchless methods handle a line under my driveway or pool deck?

That is precisely their habitat. Runs under hardscape are where trenchless economics shine brightest, since the avoided cost is not just digging but demolishing and re-pouring concrete. The survey confirms route and depth beneath the slab, the entry pits land in forgiving ground at each end, and the driveway never learns what happened underneath it.
