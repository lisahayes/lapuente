Pinpointing is a standard, not a gadget

No single instrument pinpoints. Each method on this site produces an estimate with its own error bars: tracing draws the route, correlation computes a distance, acoustic finds a surface peak, thermal frames a zone, gas marks a vent point. Pinpointing is what happens when those independent estimates are forced to agree: measurements from different physics converging on the same spot multiply confidence in a way repeating one method never can.

Our working rule is two independent confirmations before paint, three before concrete. A mark that survives cross-examination by unrelated instruments is a mark worth drilling.

The convergence sequence on a real job

A typical slab case runs the funnel: isolation testing names the guilty line, tracing draws its route through the house, correlation or thermal narrows to a segment, acoustic sweeps that segment to a peak, and, where stakes or ambiguity demand, ultrasound or tracer gas casts the deciding vote. Each stage shrinks the search space the next stage works in, which is why the sequence is fast despite its thoroughness: nobody sweeps a whole floor when the math already said which six feet matter.

Downtown’s mixed-age houses, three plumbing generations layered per lot, are where the funnel earns its keep most visibly, since assumptions there are wrong more often than anywhere.

Why the inches matter in money

Consider what the mark’s width buys. A repair opened at a verified pinpoint disturbs one patch of flooring, one square of slab, one bay of drywall, and closes the same day. Every foot of uncertainty adds cut tile, patched concrete, repainted wall, and hours, and exploratory openings that find nothing still cost full price. Driveways and pool decks raise the stakes further: hardscape forgives nothing and charges for every guess.

The detection fee, seen clearly, is a hedge against demolition. It is the cheapest part of every job it precedes, and the most expensive part of every job it was skipped on.

Our marks come with stated confidence, not theater: where physics limits certainty, congested corridors, extreme depth, we say so and price the access plan accordingly. An honest error bar beats a confident miss.

Documentation: the mark you can hand to anyone

A pinpoint deliverable outlives the visit: the located point photographed and measured off fixed references, the methods and readings that converged there, and the confidence statement, packaged so an insurance adjuster, a second contractor, or your own records can act on it without us in the room. Some clients take exactly that and manage their own repair; the report is built to survive the handoff.

Pinpoint questions before the paint

How close is a pinpoint, really?

On typical residential slab and yard cases with cooperative conditions, close enough that the repair access is a single modest opening and the pipe failure is inside it, generally a hand-span matter. Conditions set the floor: depth, saturation, and congestion widen honest error bars, and when they do, the mark comes with that stated and the access plan sized to it rather than to optimism.

Why do you insist on a second method when the first already found it?

Because single-method peaks lie in specific, known ways: sound rebroadcast by a fitting, a thermal stripe painted by a duct, a trace bent by a parallel line. Independent physics does not share those failure modes, so agreement across methods collapses the odds of a coincidence miss. The second confirmation costs minutes; a wrong hole in your slab costs the rest of the day and your patience.

Can I get just the pinpoint and handle the repair myself?

Yes, and clients do. The deliverable is designed for exactly that handoff: location, cause, readings, and photos in a form any competent contractor, or a capable owner on an accessible repair, can execute against. If you later want us for the repair after all, the file picks up where it left off. Either way, the finding is yours.
